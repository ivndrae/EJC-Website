EXPLORING NATURAL LANGUAGE PROCESSING

AND THE FUTURE OF ETHICAL AI

WITH ELINOR POOLE-DAYAN

Interview conducted by EJC Content Writer Sofia Woo

 

*The following interview has been edited and condensed for length and clarity.

 

 

 

 

How did you get interested in natural language processing and AI?

 

 

EPD: I had a vague interest in linguistics, and I was curious about the different ways that languages were structured and their origins. Eventually, as I learned more about data science and technology, I came across natural language processing (NLP). NLP is a great way for me to combine my interests in language and math.

 

 

What types of projects are you working on that involve building fair and safe AI?

 

 

EPD: Recently, I wrapped up a project about hallucination in large language models (LLMs). I was analyzing community discussions regarding sensitive topics (such as racial housing discrimination, gun violence, and police violence) and I was trying to seein the context of dialogue summarizationif models like GPT-4 would hallucinate. The dialogue topics I was studying were quite personal or vulnerable as these conversations were prevalent to the people in that specific community. I wanted to look into the ethical implications of dialogue summarization. What does it mean to ethically, faithfully, and harmlessly summarize these conversations? You want to include all of these voices but you dont want to release identifiable information. It was a very complicated and difficult process that required a lot of human evaluation.

 

Another project that Im working on right now is about syncophantic behavior in language models. Syncophantic behavior is when a model generates a response that conforms to your beliefs or requests more than it actually should be. This could be as simple as you telling the model that you think the answer is X, and the model willregardless of whether or not your answer is correcttell you that youre correct. This happens on GPT-4 and smaller models. Another example is political syncophancy. If you express your political beliefs, the model might give you an answer that conforms to your beliefs rather than something that might be more objective. One of the ways that this becomes even more problematic is that the models tend to answer less accurately if it perceives the user as uneducated. This is called sandbagging. Im looking to see how these outputs manifest when the user is an ESL speaker or of different ethnic origin and nationality.

 

Can you talk more about your interests in AI literacy?

 

 

EPD: One of my other passions outside of NLP is AI literacy and education. I took a class where the project I did with a couple of other people was developing an AI powered tool to help primarily middle school teachers create lesson plans that incorporated AI literacy. The tool helped teachers incorporate AI literacy learning objectives into their curriculum. It was a really interesting project in co-design which is really important when creating ethical AI. In our case the tool was co-designed with teachers. We interviewed teachers in every step of our design and development process. Having their feedback to inform what we built was really effective in making the tool so much better.

 

 

Given the research that you have done in this field, are there any glaring gaps or holes in AI safety and ethics that you think need to be urgently fixed?

 

 

EPD: One thing that worries me more on the industry and regulation side is the tendency towards closed models. The way that our industry (especially in the US) is built is that the incentive for someone to monetize the best models is much higher than the incentive to wait an extra twelve to twenty-four months to really properly vet models safety aspects. There is a lack of regulation regarding generative AI and there is very little investment in alignment and safety practices for LLMs. I think the best thing moving forward would be to have greater investment on the research and policy side of AI.

 

I think government investment and research grants are important to make graphics processing units (GPUs) more equitable to researchers. The leading GPUs made by companies like Nvidia are mostly owned by large private companies. This makes it really hard for academics, researchers, and independent audit organizations who want to conduct safety research and audits as they dont have nearly the financial scale to conduct these tests. Its really hard to do anything effective if were so limited by our resources. It would be great to have a huge government investment in making those resources available to those who are not making millions of dollars monetizing LLMs.

 

 

Are there any fields in AI that are overlooked but that you think will be more well known in the near future?

 

 

EPD: There are a lot of really interesting concepts in the way that you can map how humans ground their knowledge of the world. For example, we know what colors are because we have conceptual abilities that have allowed us to ground these concepts into real life. AI and LLMs largely dont have that ability (which some think is a core requirement to reach artificial general intelligence). I dont know if anyones figured this out yet but I wonder how we can use grounding (which generally manifests itself in multi-modal AI) for alignment processes. If you have an empathetic robot that can interact with the world then the robot can have these experiences that ground it as opposed to training language models in the way we currently do. I dont think were close to anything at that scale yet but I think that it would be cool to achieve this feat.
